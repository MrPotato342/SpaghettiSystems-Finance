Why Prepayment Matters

Home loans are typically structured so that interest dominates the early years of repayment. In most EMI schedules, a large portion of your initial payments goes toward interest rather than principal reduction.

This creates a compounding disadvantage: the longer the principal stays high, the more total interest you end up paying over the loan lifetime.

Prepayment directly attacks this structure by reducing the principal early. Even small lump-sum payments in the first 3–7 years can disproportionately reduce total interest outflow compared to the same amount paid later in the loan cycle.

In most cases, reducing the loan tenure is more efficient than reducing EMI. A shorter tenure reduces the compounding window, which significantly lowers total interest paid, even if monthly cash flow remains similar.

The key idea is simple: early principal reduction has exponential impact on total savings because interest is calculated on the remaining outstanding balance over time.
